# Talk: Expansion, Defense, and Exposure

Commander, the central question is not who occupies more points. It is how each expansion changes the shape of your own vulnerability. The triangular board opens from a narrow home front into a wide field: near the base, supply is short and stable; toward the center, space grows, but the front stretches.

> The more you expand, the longer your line becomes, and the easier it is to cut. The more you defend, the less space you keep, and the easier it is to be boxed in.

That is the first contradiction. Expansion gives territory, but also creates longer borders, thinner connections, and visible bridges. Defense looks stable, but it pushes you back toward the base and hands the outside line to the opponent, compressing your room to maneuver. This is the game's battlefield tension: wide strategic depth is not free, and a narrow defensive line is not a safe ending.

> The faster you expand, the more lines you expose. The more you try to hide every weakness, the more efficiency the rules force you to lose.

That is the second contradiction. Lines are not decoration. They are supply routes, and therefore attack targets. If you expand quickly, you leave unfinished long lines, bridges, and articulation points. If you insist on perfect closure, the triangle restriction, visibility rule, and move order make you pay in tempo. The rules force the player to accept that no shape can be both maximally fast and completely unexposed.

## Intuition

* Expansion and collapse are linked. A new node can enlarge territory or turn the whole front into a cuttable bridge.
* Defense and suffocation are linked. Staying near the base reduces exposure but gives away strategic depth.
* Speed and completeness are linked. Fast growth exposes lines; slow repair loses initiative.
* Attacks do not merely capture pieces. They sever connectivity to the base. What is destroyed is not just one point, but a whole structure that has lost supply.

The game is not a simple occupation race. It asks the player to balance efficiency, closure, bridges, articulation points, frontage, and future space. Its elegance is not that the rules are complicated, but that the rules close every escape route: you must expand, and you must expose yourself; you must defend, and you must leave space.
